What is Open Source Physical Security?

Date Fri 05 September 2014

The physical security of our people in the field is too important for the knowledge, tools and experience we have to be kept under the lock and key of restrictive licensing, whether intentional or not.

The year 2013 set a new record for violence against civilian aid operations, with 251 separate attacks affecting 460 aid workers.

In 2013, over half of all violent incidents occurred in the context of an ambush or roadside attack.

The hoarding of knowledge has a direct impact on the safety of our people.

The limited sharing of practice may have contributed to the narrow range of alternatives and lack of innovation within the community. It has brought the issue of road security to an intellectual dead end, with humanitarian professionals feeling that, as one put it, ‘The only way to deal with the risk is not to move.’

Aid Worker Security Report 2014

Big name organisations sending cease and desist

Security process and procedures are the core business of security companies, not NGOs

Many field workers have informal systems of sharing information, often accruing flash drives full of useful (and not so useful) documents from a variety of organisations.

Leads to inconsistencies, especially if the field is applying methodologies that are at odds with hq's expectations

Cannot be legally shared

No quality control

Reasons to Open Source

  • Leverage the knowledge and experience of the whole community to keep procedures and policies up to date and to drive innovation

  • Documents can be translated easily into other languages using services like Transifex. This enhances the safety and security of the most vulnerable of humanitarian aid workers - the locally employed.

  • Access to resources for smaller organisations who otherwise may not have had any security infrastructure at all

  • Security providers and consultants can focus on implementation rather than recreating existing documents and templates

  • Standardised terminology and format alows the rapid and accurate sharing of information

Donors need to make it a condition that any security related tools that are created are open sourced. Many donors, such as AusAid are already doing this in other areas.

Organisations need to make the resources they have developed open source and availble to the community

Individuals can contribute to resources from their own knowledge and experience.

Standardisation makes people's skills and experiences more portable between roles and other organisations

Promote innovation

Example of RTA statistic as showing outdated thinking which becomes dogma

Allows for a clear distinction between what is straight forward process and what is operational information that should be kept confidential

Small organisations can put the basics in place with limited experience
